Free Notion Templates for Freelancers (+ When to Go Paid)

March 2026 · 9 min read · By NotionKit

Let's get the obvious out of the way: yes, we sell Notion templates. And no, this isn't a bait-and-switch article where we trash free options to make you buy ours.

Free Notion templates are genuinely useful. Some of them are excellent. We use free templates ourselves for certain things. But they have real limitations — and if you're a freelancer trying to run a business, those limitations matter.

Here's an honest breakdown of the best free options, what they're good at, and where they fall short.

TL;DR: Free templates are great for getting started and learning Notion. Once you're earning €2k+/month as a freelancer, a well-built paid template (€9-19) saves you more time than it costs. The Complete Bundle (€49) replaces 6+ hours of setup.

The Best Free Notion Templates for Freelancers

1. Notion's Built-in Templates

Where to find them: Click "Templates" in any new Notion page → Browse by category

Notion has improved their first-party templates a lot since 2024. The ones worth trying:

  • Project & Tasks — Solid kanban board with basic task properties. Good for simple project management.
  • Meeting Notes — Clean and functional. One of the few built-in templates that actually works out of the box.
  • Simple Budget — Basic income/expense tracking. Fine if your finances are simple.
Verdict: Good starting points, but they're generic. None of them are designed specifically for freelancers, so you'll spend time customizing them.

2. Thomas Frank's Templates

Where to find them: thomasjfrank.com

Thomas Frank is the most well-known Notion creator, and for good reason. His Ultimate Brain template is genuinely impressive — it combines tasks, notes, and projects into one system inspired by GTD (Getting Things Done).

  • Ultimate Brain (free version) — Tasks, notes, and projects with solid database relations. The free version is limited but functional.
  • Creator's Companion — Content calendar and idea tracker. Good for YouTube creators specifically.
Verdict: High quality, but complex. The learning curve is steep. If you enjoy tinkering with Notion, you'll love it. If you just want to track clients and invoices, it's overkill.

3. Easlo's Templates

Where to find them: easlo.co

Easlo makes beautiful, minimal templates. Several are free:

  • Super Simple Task Manager — Does what it says. Clean interface, no bloat.
  • Habit Tracker — Basic daily habit tracking with checkbox properties.
Verdict: Beautiful design, but most free templates are very basic. They work well as starting points to build on.

4. Red Gregory's Templates

Where to find them: redgregory.com

Red Gregory focuses on creative and well-designed templates. Her reading list templates are particularly popular.

  • Reading List — Track books with ratings, notes, and status. One of the best free templates out there for readers.
  • Recipe Database — Not freelance-specific, but shows what a well-structured Notion database looks like.
Verdict: Excellent design sense. Worth studying even if you don't use them directly — they'll teach you how to build better databases.

5. Notion VIP (Notion Resources)

Where to find them: Various Notion community sites and Reddit r/Notion

  • Freelance Dashboard — Combines client list, project tracker, and basic invoicing. The closest free option to an all-in-one freelance system.
  • CRM Lite — Simple client database with contact info and deal stages.
Verdict: Functional for freelancers, but no sample data — you're staring at empty databases trying to figure out what goes where.

The Real Problem with Free Templates

Here's what nobody talks about in "free template" roundups. We've seen hundreds of freelancers go through the same cycle:

  1. Download 5-10 free templates — excited to "finally get organized"
  2. Spend 3-4 hours customizing — adding properties, changing views, merging pieces from different templates
  3. Hit a wall — the databases don't relate to each other, formulas are missing, and the system feels fragmented
  4. Give up or start over — back to the spreadsheet (or worse, back to tracking things in your head)

The issue isn't quality — many free templates are well-made. The issues are:

  • No sample data: You duplicate an empty database and have to figure out the intended workflow from scratch. A well-built paid template comes pre-loaded with realistic data that shows you exactly how the system works.
  • No database relationships: Free templates are usually standalone databases. But a real freelance system needs clients linked to projects linked to invoices linked to revenue. That interconnection is what makes Notion powerful — and it takes hours to set up correctly.
  • No documentation: Good paid templates include instructions — not just "what this is" but "when to use this" and "how to adapt this to your workflow." Free templates assume you'll figure it out.
  • Fragmented systems: You end up with a project tracker from one creator, an invoice tracker from another, and a habit tracker from a third. None of them talk to each other.

When Free Is Enough (Honestly)

Free templates make total sense if:

  • You're just starting freelancing and earning under €1k/month — invest your money in skills, not tools
  • You enjoy building in Notion and see the setup time as learning, not wasted time
  • You need one specific thing (like a reading list or habit tracker) that doesn't need to connect to your business system
  • You're testing whether Notion works for you before committing to a workflow

When Paid Templates Are Worth It

Paid templates make sense when:

  • Your time has a clear dollar value — if you bill €50/hour and a template saves you 4 hours of setup, a €14 template has a 14x ROI
  • You need connected systems — clients → projects → invoices → revenue all linked together
  • You want to start using it today, not spend your weekend building it
  • You've already tried free templates and hit the "fragmented system" wall
The math: If you bill €40/hour and spend 4 hours building a Notion system from free templates, that's €160 of your time. A €14-49 template that takes 15 minutes to set up saves you €150+ in opportunity cost.

Our Recommendation: Start Free, Upgrade When It Hurts

Here's what we actually tell people:

  1. Start with Notion's built-in templates — Get familiar with databases, views, and properties.
  2. Try Thomas Frank's free templates — See what a well-built system looks like.
  3. When you feel the friction — databases that don't connect, workflows that feel clunky, time wasted on manual tracking — that's when a paid template pays for itself.

Don't buy a template because someone told you to. Buy it because you've felt the pain of not having a system, and you want to skip the 4-hour DIY process.
